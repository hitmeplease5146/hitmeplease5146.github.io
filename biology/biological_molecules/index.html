Lipids

Triglycerides and phospholipids are two groups of lipid.

Triglycerides

Triglycerides are formed by the condensation of one molecule of glycerol and three molecules of fatty acid.

A condensation reaction between glycerol and a fatty acid (RCOOH) forms an ester bond.

The R-group of a fatty acid may be saturated or unsaturated.

  • A saturated fatty acid has no double bond between carbon atoms.
  • A unsaturated fatty acid has double bonds between carbon atoms

Phospholipid

  • In phospholipids, one of the fatty acids of a triglyceride is substituted by a phosphate group.
  • A phospholipid molecule have a hydrophilic head because of its charged phosphate group and two hydrophobic tails.

Phospholipids make up the bilayer of cell membranes. In a phospholipid bilayer, hydrophilic heads point outwards because they are attracted to the aqueous environment on either side. On the other hand, the hydrophobic tails point inwards because they are repelled by the aqueous environment. This acts as a hydrophobic barrier which repel water-soluble substances and stop them from passing through.

Many proteins are enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts that reduce the activation energies of many metabolic reactions

Induced-fit Model

The induced-fit model of enzyme action suggests that the active site of an enzyme changes slightly to be complementary in shape with the substrate when it binds to its substrate. This forms enzyme-substrate complex. This puts stain onto the substrate therefore distort and break/form the bonds. As a result, the activeation energy of the reaction is lowered.

Factors affecting the activities of enzymes

  1. Temperature
    • Increase in temperature increases the kinetic energy of enzymes and its substrates so they move faster and collide more frequently to form enzyme-substrate complexes to catalyse the reaction.
    • At high temperature, the hydrogen bonds which hold the tertiary structure of the enzymes are broken. This changes the tertiary structures of the enzyme so the shape of their active sites change. As a result, the active sites of enzymes are no longer complementary to their substrates so enzyme-substrate complexes cannot be formed.
  1. pH
    • All enzymes have an optimum pH value.
    • At extreme pH values, the OH- and H+ ions change the charge of the amino acids at the active site. As a result, the hydrogen and ionic bonds holding the shape of the active site break and the tertiary structure of the enzyme changes. The active site is no longer complementary in shape to its enzymes so it cannot form enzyme-substrate complexes.
  1. Substrate Concentration
    • More substrates mean there is an increased chance of enzyme and substrate colliding and form enzyme-substrate complexes. As a result, the rate of reaction increases.
    • After a certain point, the rate of activity will cease to rise regardless of any further increases in substrate levels. This is because all active sites of enzymes are occupied and saturated.
  1. Enzyme Concentration
    • More enzymes mean there is an increased chance of enzyme and substrate colliding and form enzyme-substrate complexes. As a result, the rate of reaction increases.
    • After a certain point, the rate of activity will cease to rise regardless of any further increases in enzyme levels. This is because there is not enough substrates and substrate is the limiting factor.
  1. Competitive Inhibition
    • The competitive inhibitor is structurally and chemically similar to the substrate.
    • The inhibitor binds to and blocks the active site of the enzyme thus prevents the formation of enzyme-substrate complex.
    • As the inhibitor is in competition with the substrate, its effects can be reduced by increasing substrate concentration
  1. Noncompetitive Inhibition
    • Non-competitive inhibition involves a molecule binding to an allosteric site(a site away from the active site)

Nucleic Acids

Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and ribonucleic acid (RNA) are important information-carrying molecules. In all living cells, DNA holds genetic information and RNA transfers genetic information from DNA to the ribosomes.

Ribosomes are formed from RNA and proteins.

Structure of DNA and RNA

Both DNA and RNA are polymers of nucleotides. Each nucleotide is formed from a pentose, a nitrogen-containing organic base and a phosphate group:

  • The components of a DNA nucleotide are deoxyribose, a phosphate group and one of the organic bases adenine, cytosine, guanine or thymine.
  • The components of an RNA nucleotide are ribose, a phosphate group and one of the organic bases adenine, cytosine, guanine or uracil.
  • A condensation reaction between two nucleotides forms a phosphodiester bond.

A DNA molecule is a double helix with two polynucleotide chains held together by hydrogen bonds between specific complementary base pairs.

An RNA molecule is a relatively short polynucleotide chain.

DNA replication

The semi-conservative replication of DNA ensures genetic continuity between generations of cells.

The process of semi-conservative replication of DNA in terms of :

  1. DNA helicase breaks the hydrogen bonds between the bases and unwinds the double helix.
  1. Each of the seperated parental DNA strands acts as a template. Free DNA nucleotides are attracted and pair up accourding to complementary base pairing.
  1. DNA polymerase join the nucleotides together in a condensation reaction forming phosphodiester bonds. Hydrogen bonds form between two strands of DNA.
  1. The process is described as semi-conservative as one strand is original and the other strand is new.

ATP

A single molecule of adenosine triphosphate (ATP) is a nucleotide derivative and is formed from a molecule of ribose, a molecule of adenine and three phosphate groups.

Hydrolysis of ATP to adenosine diphosphate (ADP) and an inorganic phosphate group (Pi) is catalysed by the enzyme ATP hydrolase.

  • The hydrolysis of ATP can be coupled to energy-requiring reactions within cells.
  • The inorganic phosphate released during the hydrolysis of ATP can be used to phosphorylate other compounds, often making them more reactive.

ATP is resynthesised by the condensation of ADP and Pi. This reaction is catalysed by the enzyme ATP synthase during photosynthesis, or during respiration.
